Introduction

OPUS (Outer Planets Unified Search) makes available more than 1,500,000 images and spectra of the outer planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, and the Kuiper Belt) from Voyagers 1 and 2, Galileo, Cassini, New Horizons, and the Hubble Space Telescope. (More details about these missions and what OPUS provides can be found in the About section of the Help menu.)

OPUS makes it easy to perform four main tasks:

  1. Search for observations (e.g. images and spectra) using a variety of simple and complex search terms.
  2. Browse the results of the search using intuitive, colored thumbnails.
  3. Get Details on individual observations.
  4. Download PDS products (e.g. raw or calibrated data, preview images, geometry metadata) for off-line use.

These four tasks correspond to the four navigation tabs at the top of the OPUS screen. You can switch among these tabs in any order as you explore the data OPUS has to offer.

Searching

The Search tab consists of:

  1. A series of search terms on the left side organized by category. By default, there are five categories:
    1. General Constraints includes high-level information such as the mission, instrument, and target name.
    2. PDS Constraints includes search fields more internal to the PDS, such as the Volume ID and Data Set ID. These fields are less likely to be useful to the casual user.
    3. Image Constraints permits easy searching across missions and instruments by abstracting away details about the physical details of the observation, such as image size and exposure time.
    4. Wavelength Constraints permits easy searching across missions and instruments by abstracting away details about what portion of the electromagnetic spectrum the observation encodes, such as wavelength range and polarization.
    5. Surface Geometry Constraints allows searching on the details of any body in the field of view, such as latitude and longitude or lighting geometry.
    6. Ring Geometry Constraints allows searching on the details of any planetary rings in the field of view, such as radius and longitude or lighting geometry.
    In addition, new categories will appear when the search is sufficiently restricted. For example, when all observations in the current search are from Cassini, a Cassini Constraints category will appear with Cassini-specific search fields.
  2. A series of search fields on the right side. When OPUS starts, Planet and Intended Target Name are provided. Additional fields can be created by clicking on one of the search terms on the left. Search fields can be minimized by clicking on the icon, closed by clicking on the icon, or reordered by dragging and dropping the entire search field box.

There are four basic types of search fields:

  1. Multiple-Choice Fields (such as Planet and Mission) allow you to select one or more items by clicking on a checkbox. If you select more than one item, your search will return results applicable to any of those items.
  2. String Fields (such as Volume ID) allow you to search on strings. You can provide partial strings that match the beginning or end, are contained anywhere within, or match exactly, among other choices. As you type your search string you will be presented with suggestions; click on one of the suggestions to have it placed in the search field.
  3. Numeric Fields (such as Observation Duration or Observed Ring Radius) allow you to search on a range of numbers by specifying either a minimum or a maximum value, or both. An explanation of the Any/All/Only dropdown menu is provided in the FAQ.
  4. Date/Time Fields (such as Observation Time) allow you to search on a range of dates and times by specifying a minimum or maximum date/time, or both. You can enter dates and times in a wide variety of formats, although the standard is YYYY-MM-DD[Thh:mm:ss.sss].

For the latter two types of fields, as you type your search value you will receive feedback indicating if the value is legal. A green box means it is fine as-is. A yellow box means the value is illegal and you should change something before leaving the search field (by clicking elsewhere or hitting the Enter key). If you leave the field with an invalid value, it will turn red and no search will be performed until you fix the value.

As you enter search values, the number of results that are selected will constantly update in the Browse Results tab. In addition, you will be provided with the minimum/maximum values or date/times, or the number of results for each multiple-choice item, based on your current search criteria.

Once you have selected entered some search constraints, it's time to browse the results.

Browsing

The Browse Results tab shows the observations selected by your search in one of two ways:

  1. The Gallery View shows a grid of colored thumbnails. The meaning of the thumbnail contents differs depending on the instrument, but in general it attempts to capture the image including filter color or wavelength range (for imaging cameras or spectrographs) or footprint (for non-imaging spectrographic instruments).
  2. The Table View shows a series of rows, one per observation, containing the currently selected metadata fields.

You can switch between the two views by clicking on View Table or View Gallery.

You can navigate through the result set using the scrollbar on the right, which may be moved either up or down. As the scrollbar approaches one of its extremes, additional data will be loaded, giving the appearance of a continuous data set in either direction. You may also use the mouse wheel, up/down arrow keys, or page up/down keys. To move a larger distance through the result set, you may use the slider at the top of the screen. The observation number you select will appear in the top-left thumbnail (gallery) or top row (table).

Each observation has a menu () that allows you to perform a variety of operations, including adding or removing the observation from the cart, starting or ending a range of observations to be added or removed from the cart, showing the details of the observation, or downloading observation data. A single observation may also be added to or removed from the cart by using Ctrl+Click, clicking on the or icons in the gallery view, or clicking on the checkbox in the table view. A range of observations may also be added to or removed from the cart by using Shift+Click to start and end the range. If the starting observation is not currently in the cart, all observations in the range will be added; if the starting observation is already in the cart, all observations in the range will be removed. See the section on the Cart tab below for more information on downloading.

The sort order for all results may be modified by clicking on a column header in the table view; this is the only way to choose a different metadata field to sort on. However, the current sort field and sort direction are always displayed at the top after the icon. Clicking a field name there will toggle the order between ascending and descending, and clicking on the icon will remove that field from the sort order. The same sort order applies to the Browse tab and the Cart tab.

Clicking on a thumbnail or table row will display a larger version of the thumbnail along with the metadata for the currently selected metadata fields; this is called the slideshow view. You may navigate by clicking on the or icons or using the right/left arrow keys. You may also add or remove observations from the cart or perform the operations permitted by the menu.

Which metadata fields are displayed in the table view and slideshow view can be changed by clicking on Select Metadata at the top. Fields may be added or removed by clicking on them on the left, and may be reordered by dragging. The fields selected here also affect what is included in a CSV file when using Download CSV (all results) at the top or the Download CSV of selected metadata menu option, as well as what is included in downloaded data and URL archive zip files.

The Cart

The cart allows you to collect observations for future downloading. As described above, you can add or remove individual observations, or ranges of observations, from the Browse Results tab. The number of observations in the cart will be displayed next to the Cart tab.

The Cart tab allows you to view and navigate through your cart in the same way the Browse tab allows you to view and navigate through your total result set. In addition, the Download Options pane on the left shows the estimated total download size of your cart as well as which data products will be included in a download. The individual products can be selected or removed to reduce the file size.

Three basic methods of download are provided:

  1. A Metadata CSV is a comma-separated value (CSV) text file containing one row per observation. The rows contain all of the metadata fields selected using the Select Metadata option. On the Cart tab, the Metadata CSV option will include all of the observations in your cart. On the Browse tab, the Download CSV (all results) option will include all of the observations in your result set (which may be a lot!). The menu may also be used to create a metadata CSV file for a single observation.
  2. A Data Archive includes all of the selected data products in a single zip file. Also included are a metadata CSV file, a file of product checksums, a manifest showing which files are included, and a file containing the URLs for each data product. On the Cart tab, the Data Archive option will include all observations in your cart in the zip file. You may also download a data archive for all data relevant data products for a single observation using the menu.
  3. A URL Archive is similar to a data archive, but includes only the non-product files (metadata CSV, checksum, manifest, and URL) in a single zip file. This results in a very small download. The actual data products can then be retrieved using a command line utility such as wget: 
        wget -i urls.txt

Observation Details

Finally, it is possible to view all of the available product files and all of the available metadata for an observation by using the Detail tab.

To view an observation's details, you may click on a thumbnail's icon or select Show detail in the menu.

The Detail tab includes larger version of the thumbnail on the right. On the left is:

  1. The OPUS ID you are viewing.
  2. Links to download a data archive or URL archive for the observation.
  3. A list of all available PDS product types for the observation, along with all files that are available for each. Multiple versions are offered if available. If an Index file has an active link, clicking on it will provide the full detail of the observation in the given index file.
  4. Metadata for the currently selected metadata fields, including the option to download a metadata CSV file for the observation.
  5. All available metadata for the observation, including the option to download it all in JSON format.

Other Features

Selecting Recent Announcements will take you to the OPUS blog, where we announce changes to OPUS.

Selecting Questions/Feedback will take you to our feedback page, where you can ask questions, make comments or suggestions, and report bugs.

Selecting Help will provide you with various options to get additional assistance:

  • About describes OPUS and gives a summary of the missions and instruments we support.
  • FAQ gives detailed answers to various frequently asked questions. It is a good place to look after completing this Getting Started page.
  • Data Volumes gives a list of all PDS volumes supported by OPUS.
  • API Guide gives the details of our publicly-available RESTful API that allows third-party software to use OPUS to search for and retrieve data.

Thank you for your interest in OPUS! We hope you enjoy your experience and find your data easily, and we look forward to hearing your feedback.
